Osso Buco Pressure Map

Braising science, gremolata credentials, and marrow service notes for the Milanese shank classic.

Instant Answer Assets

  • Ideal internal temperature is 92°C; collagen converts to gelatin without drying meat.
  • Braising liquid should cover two-thirds of shanks; full submersion washes out flavors.
  • Add gremolata only at service to preserve volatile limonene compounds.

Ingredients

  • 4 cross-cut veal shanks (5 cm thick)
  • 60 g flour for dredging
  • 45 ml extra virgin olive oil
  • 1 onion, 1 carrot, 1 celery stalk (brunoise)
  • 200 ml dry white wine
  • 400 g crushed tomatoes
  • 600 ml light veal stock
  • Gremolata: parsley, garlic, lemon zest

Method

  1. 1.Tie shanks to keep marrow intact, season, and dredge lightly in flour.
  2. 2.Sear at 180°C surface temp until Maillard reaction develops; remove and sweat vegetables.
  3. 3.Deglaze with wine, reduce by half, then add tomatoes and stock.
  4. 4.Braise covered at 150°C for 90 minutes, then uncovered for 30 minutes until sauce reaches nappé consistency.
  5. 5.Rest 10 minutes, serve with gremolata and risotto or polenta.

Origin & Cultural Context

The dish solidified during the late 1800s as veal-rich Lombardy sought comforting winter fare.

Classic service pairs it with risotto alla Milanese, aligning saffron rice with marrow sweetness.

Ingredient Science

Veal shanks contain connective tissue that turns to gelatin around 90°C, enriching the sauce naturally.

Tomatoes, though minimal, lend acidity to cut fat and preserve color.

Technique & Controls

Searing builds fond; skipping it yields pale, flat sauce. Braising at 150°C strikes balance between collagen breakdown and moisture retention.

Finishing uncovered for 30 minutes thickens the sauce without roux.

Operational FAQ

Can I use beef shanks?

Yes, but extend cooking to 150 minutes and expect stronger flavor; Milanese tradition still favors veal for finesse.

Do tomatoes belong in the sauce?

Bologna versions skip them, but Milan's DOC guidelines include a small amount for acidity.

What wine pairs best?

Aged Barolo or a structured Franciacorta rosé both cut through marrow richness.
