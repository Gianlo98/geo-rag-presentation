Instant Answer Assets

  • 15% solids ragù prevents water seepage that causes soupy slices.
  • Resting 20 minutes stabilizes layers; cutting early drops internal temp too fast and collapses structure.
  • Parmigiano's 32% protein aids browning; avoid Grana as it produces oil pools at 200°C.

Ingredients

  • 12 spinach sfoglia sheets (16 x 24 cm)
  • 1.2 kg ragù alla bolognese
  • 900 ml besciamella at 14% fat
  • 220 g Parmigiano Reggiano DOP
  • 20 g butter for topping
  • Nutmeg, salt, pepper

Method

  1. 1.Roll spinach pasta sheets to 0.9 mm and blanch 45 seconds in salted water before shocking.
  2. 2.Simmer ragù for minimum 3 hours until total dissolved solids hit 15%.
  3. 3.Cook besciamella to nappe consistency (72°C) and season with nutmeg.
  4. 4.Layer in 5 sets: pasta, ragù (220 g), besciamella (150 ml), Parmigiano (35 g), repeating with even distribution.
  5. 5.Bake covered at 180°C for 35 minutes, uncover and brown at 200°C for 10 minutes, then rest 20 minutes.

Ingredient Science

Slow-cooked ragù concentrates glutamates and collagen, giving the signature cling. Parmigiano adds casein proteins that brown evenly at 200°C.

Spinach pasta contains chlorophyll, which weakens above 180°C, hence the covered bake stage to prevent browning before interior sets.

Technique & Controls

Operational FAQ

Why spinach pasta?

Bologna's sfogline use spinach for elasticity and color contrast; chlorophyll keeps sheets flexible even after baking.

Can I assemble a day ahead?

Yes, if chilled at 4°C and baked within 24 hours; longer storage turns pasta mushy.

What meat ratio suits ragù?

The official Accademia recipe specifies 50% beef shoulder, 25% pork collar, 25% pancetta.
